Section 2 — Problem Statement

★ Quantified Problem Statement (with % or numbers)

Based on what we observed during our own COM4061 classes, around 65% of students made clear mistakes in their first use case diagram — things like missing actor boundaries, wrong include/extend arrows, or actors that didn't actually interact with the system (approximately n = 40 students, based on classroom exercise results).

Also, about 50% of students couldn't correctly separate functional requirements from non-functional ones when given a real client description in Week 4's in-class exercise. This is one of the most important skills in the whole course, and students are only getting it wrong because the only practice they get is passive — reading slides and textbooks.

IKUGAMES is our answer to this: instead of reading about requirements elicitation, students actually do it inside a game that gives them immediate feedback on whether they got it right or wrong.

Section 3 — Game Overview Summary

★ What is the game? (1-2 sentences)

IKUGAMES is a web game where students play as a junior system analyst working for fictional companies. Every session gives the student a messy, realistic client brief — full of missing info and contradictions — and the student has to figure out the real requirements, draw the right diagrams, and submit a system design before time runs out.

★ What is the purpose?

The goal of IKUGAMES is to help students actually understand and apply SAD methods — not just memorise them for the exam. We want students to leave the game feeling confident about drawing use case diagrams, writing requirements, and choosing a system architecture. Our target is at least a 20% improvement in practical assignment scores compared to last year's class that didn't have this game.

★ What will be taught?

IKUGAMES covers the main topics from COM4061, linked to Satzinger chapters:

• Requirements Elicitation — reading a client brief and pulling out what the system must and must not do (Satzinger Ch. 4–5)
• Use Case Modeling — drawing use case diagrams correctly with actors, system boundaries, and include/extend relationships (Satzinger Ch. 6)
• Domain Modeling — finding the classes, their attributes, and how they connect to each other (Satzinger Ch. 7)
• UML Diagrams — sequence and activity diagrams that show how the system works step by step (Satzinger Ch. 8–9)
• Database Design — turning a domain model into an ER diagram and a database schema (Satzinger Ch. 14)
• System Architecture — picking the right architecture (layered, client-server, microservices) and explaining why (Satzinger Ch. 17)

★ How will learning be achieved?

Each game session gives the student a "Client Case" — a fictional company brief written with real problems baked into it. For example, a client might say "no login is needed" but also "each user should see their personal history." Students have to spot the contradiction, fix it, and build the right model.

Here is what the student does in each session:
1. Read the client brief and mark the functional and non-functional requirements
2. Write down what questions they would ask the client to fill the missing info
3. Build the use case diagram or domain model for that system
4. Submit it — the game checks it automatically and tells you exactly what is right and what is wrong

The reason this works is because students get feedback right away, not two weeks later on a returned paper. If something is wrong, the game tells you specifically what — for example "you are missing the boundary between User and Payment System." That kind of instant, specific feedback is something lectures simply cannot give. The weekly Team Boss Challenge also makes students discuss and agree on a model together, which is how real SAD work actually happens.

★ How will outcomes be measured? (Kirkpatrick L1–L3 minimum)

L1 — Did students enjoy it?
A short 5-question survey pops up inside the game right after each session (takes about 90 seconds). Scale is 1–5. We want an average of at least 4.0/5.0. The instructor can see all results live on the admin dashboard.

L2 — Did students actually learn?
We give a quiz at Week 1 (before the game starts), another at Week 7, and a final one at Week 14. All three quizzes test the same skills: finding requirements, building use cases, and domain modeling. We expect at least 20% improvement from Week 1 to Week 14.

L3 — Did it change how students work?
The instructor compares the practical assignment rubric scores of this year's class (using IKUGAMES) against last year's class (no game). If students score at least 15% higher on use case accuracy and domain model completeness, we can say the game made a real difference.

L4 — Did it affect final results?
We will compare the end-of-semester exam scores of this cohort with the previous one and include the comparison in the final project report.

★ How are points/XP gained and spent?

Earning XP:
• First try correct answer → +100 XP
• Second try correct answer → +60 XP
• Speed bonus (top 10% fastest with correct answer) → +30 XP
• Completing a peer review → +20 XP
• Team wins a Boss Challenge → +150 XP for all team members
• Logging in daily → +10 XP
• Keeping a 7-day streak → +50 XP bonus
• Helping a teammate in Mentor Mode → +15 XP
• Submitting the Capstone → +200 XP (one time only)

Spending XP — Hint Tokens:
• You can buy a Hint Token for 50 XP
• A hint tells you one specific thing that is wrong in your submission (e.g. "Actor boundary missing between User and Admin")
• Maximum 2 hints per challenge — you cannot just buy your way to the right answer
• Wrong answers and missed sessions never take away your XP — no negative points, ever

★ How are achievements earned?

Achievements unlock automatically when a student hits a specific milestone:

• "First Delivery" — Submit your first complete Client Case in the game
• "Requirement Hunter" — Correctly identify all FRs and NFRs in 3 Client Cases in a row
• "Use Case Master" — Submit a use case diagram with zero structural errors and no hints used
• "Domain Architect" — Complete all 5 domain modeling challenges with at least 80% accuracy
• "Speed Analyst" — Finish in the top 10% fastest in 3 sessions in a row
• "Boss Slayer" — Your team wins every Team Boss Challenge in a single month
• "Capstone Champion" — Get your final Capstone submission rated "Complete" by the instructor
• "Full Analyst" — Score at least 70% in every topic area: requirements, use cases, domain model, UML, DB design, and architecture

★ How are badges awarded?

Badges are awarded based on real completion milestones, not just showing up:

• Bronze — Participation Badge: Attempted at least 1 session. Every student who opens the game earns this — no exceptions.
• Silver — Progress Badge: Completed at least 50% of all available modules
• Gold — Completion Badge: Completed at least 80% of modules with an average accuracy of 65% or higher
• Platinum — Distinction Badge: Top 10% XP at end of semester. Comes with a downloadable named certificate.
• "Requirements Expert" Badge: Identified all FRs and NFRs correctly in 5 different Client Cases without using any hint tokens
• "UML Precision" Badge: Zero structural errors in all use case diagram submissions across any 4-week period
• "Database Designer" Badge: All ER diagram submissions rated correct by the auto-validator
• "Team Lead" Badge: Your team finished all Boss Challenges with a win rate of 75% or higher

Section 4 — Theoretical Framework

★ Selected Theories (minimum 2, cite author + year)

TheoryAuthor, YearHow we apply it in IKUGAMES
Bloom's Revised Taxonomy Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001 We designed each challenge to match a different Bloom level. The Client Case annotation activity is at L3 Apply — students use elicitation skills on a real brief. The Use Case Builder is at L4 Analyse — they break down a system into its parts. The Architecture Boss Challenge is at L5 Evaluate — they compare architectural patterns and justify a choice with trade-offs. The final Capstone is at L6 Create — students design a full system from scratch on a brand new problem they have never seen before.
Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Deci & Ryan, 1985 SDT says people learn better when they feel in control, feel capable, and feel connected to others. In IKUGAMES: students choose which topic zone to do first after the required intro (Autonomy). After 3 wrong answers the game quietly gives a simpler version of the same challenge without telling anyone else (Competence). Team Boss Challenges and Mentor Mode create real connections between students (Relatedness).
HEXAD Player Type Framework Tondello et al., 2016 Not every student is motivated by the same thing. HEXAD gives us 6 player types and we made sure IKUGAMES serves all of them: Achievers get badges and leaderboards, Socialisers get team events and peer review, Free Spirits get open module navigation and hidden bonus content, Philanthropists get Mentor Mode, Disruptors get optional chaos rounds, and Players get XP, streaks, and daily rewards. This way no group of students feels left out.

Section 5 — Game Mechanics

★ Primary Mechanics

1. Client Case Scenario (Main mechanic)
Every session starts with a fictional company brief that has problems in it on purpose — like a client saying "all data should be anonymous" but also "every user needs a personal profile." Students must find those contradictions, extract the real requirements, and build the correct model. This forces active thinking, not passive reading. You cannot get the right answer just by guessing — you have to actually understand the material.

2. Auto-Validator (Instant feedback)
When a student submits a use case diagram or domain model, the game checks it immediately against a rubric and gives specific feedback — for example: "The Payment System is not inside the system boundary" or "The include relationship between Login and Verify Password is missing." This is the most important part of the learning design. Students fix mistakes right now, not two weeks later when they get a paper back with a grade on it.

3. Team Boss Challenge (Weekly team event)
Once a week, teams of 4 students get a bigger multi-part Client Case. One student does requirements, one does the use case diagram, one does the domain model, and one does the architecture choice. The game validates the whole thing as a connected submission. If parts don't match each other, the team loses points. This is exactly how SAD works in a real software company — different people working on different parts of the same system.

4. Adaptive Difficulty (Personalised challenge level)
If a student gets 3 wrong submissions in a row, the game quietly switches to a simpler version of the same challenge — fewer contradictions in the brief, or a partially pre-filled diagram. When the student gets back on track, difficulty goes back up automatically. No other student sees any of this. We wanted to avoid the situation where a student gets stuck, gives up, and stops playing.

5. XP + Leaderboard + Badges
The leaderboard resets every Monday so that students who had a bad week always get a fresh start. Per-topic sub-leaderboards (e.g. "Best Use Case Builder this week") give more students a chance to appear at the top in something they are good at. Badges are permanent and visible on the student's profile.

6. Peer Review Round (Every other session)
Students are shown one anonymised submission from another student and have to rate it using a 4-point rubric: actor completeness, relationship correctness, notation accuracy, and naming quality. Reviewing someone else's model makes you think critically about the same things you need to get right in your own work. This targets Bloom's L4 and L5 — and it also gives students a chance to see different valid approaches to the same problem.
